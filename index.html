Introducing VGuard®

We present the first electrostimulating medical device targeting brain structures responsible for memory consolidation. VGuard is the first non-invasive home use electronic device dedicated specially for patients suffering from Alzheimer's disease to boost memory and make their life easier.

The VGuard device is dedicated for patients with mild cognitive impairment (MCI), specially for patients with Alzheimer’s disease or dementia above 60 years old.

Research

About Alzheimer's disease

As Alzheimer's is a progressive neurological disease, symptoms worsen over time, but quality of life and expected lifetime after diagnosis may vary depending on may factors. Neurodegenerative changes in the brain begin years before any noticible symptoms of the Alzheimer's disease. First symptoms are often mistakenly attributed to aging or stress. Detailed neuropsychological tests may reveal mild cognitive impairment up to eight years before the patient meets clinical criteria for AD diagnosis. The most visible is the loss of fresh memory in the form of difficulties in recalling recently remembered facts and inability to memorize new information.

Short story

Already ancient Greeks and Romans associated aging with the deterioration of memory, but they did not know possible reasons. In 1906 the German psychiatrist, Dr. Alois Alzheimer for the first time described the case of a disease in woman who had died with undefined neurological symptoms including memory problems along with language impairment and unpredictable behavior. The brain autopsy showed extensive atrophy of the cortex (pathologically thin gray matter) and two types of abnormal deposits inside (now known as amyloid plaques) and between neurons (now called neurofibrillary tangles). Till today this neurodegenerative disease is carrying his name.

Global disease

Statistics say that AD is the 6th leading cause of death in the USA. One out of ten elderly above 65 years old suffer from Alzheimer’s disease. 1 in 3 seniors dies with dementia. According to World Alzheimer Report 2018 50 million patients are living with dementia worlwide. As people live longer the number of seniors suffering from dementia is rising rapidly all over the world. By 2050, this population will triple. Currently there is no effective treatment of Alzheimer’s disease. Applied pharmacological therapies do not bring the expected result. Ongoing research put an efford to find better treatment to delay progression of the disease.
